## Context

Terminal data flows from the pty-host to the renderer via either IPC or a MessagePort (direct, faster path). Both paths use an ACK/watermark system: the renderer ACKs received bytes, and the pty-host pauses sending when unACKed bytes exceed the high watermark (128 KB) until they drop below the low watermark (32 KB).

## Problem

During high-throughput output (agent streaming, build logs), the renderer-side watermarks were not providing actual backpressure. The pty-host kept sending at wire speed regardless of how fast xterm was consuming data.

## Root cause

On the MessagePort path, ACK was sent in the `message` event handler — as soon as the data arrived in the renderer, before xterm had parsed or rendered it. The pty-host saw ACKs immediately and kept filling the queue. The IPC path correctly defers ACK to the `terminal.write()` callback (after xterm consumption). The two paths had diverged in their ACK timing.

Fix: defer MessagePort ACK to the `terminal.write()` callback, matching IPC path semantics. The byte count used for ACK is taken from the original pty-host value in the message, not re-encoded from the string.

## Lesson

When two transport paths share the same flow-control contract (ACK/watermark), their ACK timing must be identical. ACKing on receipt vs. ACKing after consumption produces completely different backpressure behaviour — the difference between watermarks that are effective and watermarks that are bypassed entirely.

When adding a fast transport path (MessagePort) alongside an existing one (IPC), explicitly verify that all protocol semantics (not just data forwarding) are preserved.

## Applies when

- Any new data transport path that participates in the ACK/watermark flow control system.
- Debugging "terminal freezes" or "pty-host flooding renderer" — check ACK timing on both transport paths.
