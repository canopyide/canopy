## Context

Putting the machine to sleep and waking it later triggered two distinct failure modes: (1) health-check timers saw a huge wall-clock gap and declared healthy PTYs dead, and (2) long-running processes sometimes emitted a backlog of buffered output on wake that saturated the main→renderer IPC channel and hung the UI.

## Problem

Sleep/wake is not a single clean event. A laptop can suspend and resume repeatedly in quick succession (lid opened briefly while carrying it). Naive `powerMonitor.on("resume")` handlers that immediately re-enable polling get invoked against stale state from a suspend that was superseded before the resume handler ran. And on the PTY side, a single fat burst of buffered output is indistinguishable from a runaway `yes` loop — both can wedge IPC.

## Root cause

Two orthogonal gaps: no sleep-aware pause for services that care about wall-clock time, and no rate-limited backpressure on the PTY→renderer channel. Either alone causes symptoms; together they amplify each other during wake storms.

## Lesson

Register `powerMonitor.on("suspend")` and `on("resume")` handlers in `main.ts` and fan them out to any service that: runs health checks against wall-clock timestamps (`PtyClient`), polls filesystem or git state (`WorkspaceService`, `WorktreeMonitor`), or holds pending timers. **Debounce the resume handler** with a short stabilization delay (~2 s) so rapid suspend/resume cycles collapse to a single resume. Track a "current cycle id" so stale resumes can be discarded.

For PTY output, implement a per-terminal circuit breaker in `PtyManager`: measure bytes/second over a short window, pause the PTY reader when a threshold is exceeded (e.g. 5 MB/s), and require a cooldown of sustained low output before resuming. Without the cooldown, the breaker oscillates — it pauses, output drains, it resumes, the next chunk immediately trips it again.

## Applies when

Any long-lived Electron app that holds native resources across sleep: PTYs, file watchers, git polling, network connections with keepalives. Always test with the lid-open-lid-close-quickly pattern, not just a single clean sleep. Always check whether your "resume" handler assumes it's the only one that ran since suspend.
