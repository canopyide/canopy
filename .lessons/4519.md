## Context

Batch-creating worktrees (e.g. bulk-create dialog) failed at exactly 10 worktrees every time. Worktrees 1–10 succeeded; anything above 10 entered retry/backoff loops.

## Problem

`worktree:create` used `checkRateLimit()` — a synchronous function that immediately throws `"Rate limit exceeded"` when the 10-call-per-10s window is exhausted.

Two compounding factors made it worse:

1. The `worktree:create` channel is grouped under the shared `"gitOps"` rate-limit category alongside `worktree:delete`, `worktree:create-for-task`, `git:get-file-diff`, `git:list-commits`, etc. Any git operation during the batch consumed worktree-creation quota.
2. An async `waitForRateLimitSlot()` helper already existed in `electron/ipc/utils.ts` but was unused for this handler — the wrong variant was chosen at the time of initial implementation.

The renderer-side dialog retried on error with exponential backoff (3–30s), so worktrees _eventually_ created, masking the issue as slow UX rather than a hard failure.

## Root cause

`checkRateLimit` (synchronous throw) was used where `waitForRateLimitSlot` (async queue/drain) was needed. For batch operations that genuinely need to exceed the window limit sequentially, the queue variant is always correct.

## Lesson

When implementing IPC rate limits, prefer `waitForRateLimitSlot` over `checkRateLimit` for any handler that may be called in batches. A synchronous throw gives the caller no choice but to error-handle and retry; an async queue naturally serialises callers and drains without errors.

Be aware that shared rate-limit categories (like `"gitOps"`) mean unrelated handlers compete for the same budget. If a handler is batch-sensitive, consider giving it a dedicated category or a higher per-window limit.

## Applies when

- Adding or modifying IPC handlers that are invoked in bulk (batch creates, bulk deletes, etc.)
- Choosing between `checkRateLimit` and `waitForRateLimitSlot` in `electron/ipc/utils.ts`
- Diagnosing failures that occur at a suspiciously round count (equal to the rate-limit window)
